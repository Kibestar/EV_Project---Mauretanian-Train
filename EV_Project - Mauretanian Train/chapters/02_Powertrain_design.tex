\section{Design of the Powertrain}
The design of the BEV Mauritanian Iron Core Train will consist of three locomotives, carrying the batteries, motors, and power conversion hardware. In this section, the design of the electric powertrain will be discussed: from the battery and the motor controllers to the motors and the transmission, including the auxiliary power used onboard. This will be done based on design trade-offs, calculations, and simulated results. 

\subsection{Energy Source}
FLXdrive is the perfect example of a full battery-electric locomotive, carrying a total capacity of up to 7 MWh, showing that our ideas are feasible \cite{FLXdrive}. 
\newline Starting on a cell level, different chemistries were considered. The main objectives are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The cells should have a long lifecycle, as this train will drive the whole day, every day. Any downtime directly results in a loss of income for every party involved.
    \item The cells should be able to operate in rough temperature conditions. During the day, temperatures can rise to a stunning 50°C, whereas they might fall below 0°C during a winter night.
\end{itemize}
LCO batteries have a high power density and a somewhat short lifetime. Since the design is not optimizing for weight or volume, LCO batteries were discarded. The comparison was ultimately made between LFP and LTO battery cells. LFP is broadly used for EVs and energy storage applications, even though its characteristic flat SOC curve makes it difficult to track the battery level. LTO is known for its extreme temperature endurance, fast charging capabilities, and unmatched lifespan. Even though LFP is cheaper to purchase, the longer lifecycle of LTO will make that chemistry cheaper in the long term than LFP. The longer lifetime (30,000 cycles), together with the better temperature performance, made us choose the Lithium Titanate Oxide Battery Cell - LTO 2.3V 40Ah cylindrical cell \cite{LTO}. The cylindrical cell was chosen, rather than the pouch cell, as it is a safer option in terms of robustness, and the weight reduction from the pouch cell is not part of our requirements. 
\newline The total onboard energy required is 12 MWh (see section ...). This will be equally divided over the three locomotives, with each carrying a battery of 4 MWh. To reach the 800 V DC bus voltage, 348 cells will be placed in series. A total of 125 parallel connections will be made, resulting in a capacity of 5000 Ah. A single cell is estimated at the price of 39 euros \cite{price}. The total amount of money for all 43,500 cells accumulates to 5 million euros.
\newline The total cell weight will be 54,375 kg per battery, with a volume of 30 m$^3$ (including terminals). At the cell level, the 4MWh capacity per locomotive yields an energy-to-weight ratio of approximately 73.5Wh/kg. Given the LTO cell's 10C discharge capability, the theoretical maximum power output is 40MW per locomotive, resulting in a power-to-weight ratio of 736W/kg. There is also enough space for the battery in the locomotive. Regarding the placement, the battery packs will be housed centrally, occupying the space traditionally reserved for the diesel engine. The modules will be securely mounted in shock-resistant racks.
\newline The LTO cell can handle charge and discharge rates of 10C, according to its datasheet \cite{LTO}. The highest energy requirement to fully charge the battery occurs at stop 5 (see section ...). The energy required is almost 10 MWh over a timespan of 30 minutes, so the peak charge power will be 20 MW. Therefore, the current will reach a maximum of 12,800 A (taking the discharge cut-off voltage of 1.5 V into account), or 2.56C, staying well below the current limit. Regenerative braking also won't deliver higher charge rates, since the downhill gradient is very shallow and the braking distance is very long.
\newline As this application integrates a BEV, the tank-to-wheel emissions are zero. Furthermore, since the electricity generation is completely decoupled from any grid and 100\% of the energy is generated by solar power, the well-to-tank emissions are also zero. Thus, the expected well-to-wheel emissions are zero.
